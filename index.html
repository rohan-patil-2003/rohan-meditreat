Treatment Overview

Total Knee Replacement (TKR), also known as knee arthroplasty, is a surgical procedure to replace a damaged, worn, or diseased knee with an artificial joint. This procedure is typically recommended for patients suffering from severe knee pain and disability due to osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, or post-traumatic arthritis.

The surgery involves removing the damaged bone and cartilage from the thighbone, shinbone, and kneecap, and replacing it with an artificial joint made of metal alloys, high-grade plastics, and polymers. The goal is to relieve pain and restore function to the knee joint.

With advances in surgical techniques and materials, knee replacement surgery has become one of the most successful procedures in orthopedics, with over 90% of patients experiencing significant pain relief and improved mobility.

Quick Facts

  • Surgery Duration: 1-2 hours
  • Hospital Stay: 3-5 days
  • Recovery Time: 6-12 weeks
  • Success Rate: Over 90%
  • Anesthesia: General or Spinal

Eligibility for Treatment

You may be a candidate for total knee replacement if you experience:

  • Severe knee pain that limits everyday activities such as walking, climbing stairs, and getting in and out of chairs
  • Moderate or severe knee pain while resting, either day or night
  • Chronic knee inflammation and swelling that doesn't improve with rest or medications
  • Knee deformity ‚Äî a bowing in or out of your knee
  • Failure to substantially improve with other treatments such as anti-inflammatory medications, cortisone injections, lubricating injections, physical therapy, or other surgeries

Preparation Before Treatment

Proper preparation is crucial for a successful knee replacement surgery. Here's what you need to do:

  • Complete all pre-operative tests including blood tests, X-rays, and ECG
  • Undergo a thorough medical evaluation to ensure you're fit for surgery
  • Stop taking certain medications as advised by your doctor (especially blood thinners)
  • Arrange for someone to help you at home during recovery
  • Prepare your home for recovery by removing tripping hazards
  • Practice using crutches or a walker before surgery
  • Stop smoking at least 4 weeks before surgery to promote better healing

About Treatment

The knee replacement procedure typically follows these steps:

  • Anesthesia: You'll receive either general anesthesia or spinal anesthesia before the procedure begins
  • Incision: The surgeon makes an incision over the knee to access the joint
  • Bone Preparation: Damaged cartilage and bone are removed from the thigh bone, shin bone, and kneecap
  • Implant Placement: The artificial joint components are attached to the prepared bone surfaces
  • Closure: The incision is closed with stitches or staples and covered with a dressing

Modern techniques include minimally invasive surgery, computer-assisted surgery, and robotic-assisted surgery, which can result in smaller incisions, less pain, and faster recovery.

Post-Treatment Care

After surgery, proper care is essential for optimal recovery:

  • Pain management with prescribed medications
  • Physical therapy starting within 24 hours of surgery
  • Wound care to prevent infection
  • Gradual increase in mobility and weight-bearing activities
  • Use of assistive devices (walker, crutches) as recommended
  • Regular follow-up appointments with your surgeon
  • Blood clot prevention measures including medication and compression devices

Warning Signs

Contact your doctor immediately if you experience:

  • Fever above 101¬∞F
  • Increased redness or swelling
  • Drainage from the incision
  • Chest pain or shortness of breath
  • Severe pain not relieved by medication

Treatment Recovery Tips

Follow these tips for a smooth recovery:

  • Exercise regularly: Perform prescribed physical therapy exercises to strengthen muscles and improve flexibility
  • Maintain a healthy weight: Excess weight puts additional stress on your new knee
  • Avoid high-impact activities: Activities like running or jumping may damage your knee implant
  • Take precautions against falls: Use handrails, wear supportive shoes, and remove hazards from your home
  • Monitor for infection: Keep the surgical site clean and watch for signs of infection
  • Stay active: Low-impact activities like walking, swimming, and cycling are beneficial

Treatment FAQs

How long does the knee joint last after surgery? +
Most knee replacements last 15-20 years or more with proper care. Factors affecting longevity include your weight, activity level, and overall health. Advances in materials and surgical techniques continue to improve implant durability.
When can I return to normal activities after surgery? +
Most patients can resume light activities within 3-6 weeks and return to most normal activities within 3 months. Full recovery typically takes 6-12 months. Your surgeon will provide specific guidance based on your progress.
Can I do activities like hiking and biking after surgery? +
Yes, low-impact activities like walking, hiking on even terrain, swimming, and cycling are generally recommended after full recovery. However, high-impact activities like running, jumping, or contact sports should be avoided to protect the implant.
What are the risks associated with knee replacement? +
Like any surgery, knee replacement carries some risks including infection, blood clots, nerve damage, implant wear or loosening, and continued pain. However, serious complications are rare, and the success rate exceeds 90%. Your surgical team will take precautions to minimize these risks.
